# Management & Leadership Mid-Semester Report — Operations

**Ahsan | Georgia Tech | Spring 2026**

---

## Define what recurring tasks you are doing within your project group.

My recurring tasks center on maintaining the operations infrastructure for the HAAG Spatial Camera Trap project. Each week, I collect individual weekly reports from the research team (Neelima Srivastava, Kefei Yan, John Hideo, and nominally Aparna and Andrew), synthesize their content, and integrate the data into the Operations Tracker—an Excel workbook I built and maintain using openpyxl. The tracker has a per-week sheet structure with assigned tasks (handed down by our computational advisor Jacquie Carroll), individual report summaries, and key findings, plus a Dashboard sheet that tracks submission status across all weeks. I also update the Project Gantt chart as milestones shift. Beyond the tracker, I monitor whether all team members are submitting their reports on time and flag gaps. The team communicates via GitHub and Slack after transitioning off Basecamp earlier in the semester.

## How are you assessing your role as the project manager and its effect on the overall project?

I assess my effectiveness primarily through two lenses: whether the team has a clear, up-to-date picture of project status at any given time, and whether submission compliance is improving. The Operations Tracker and Dashboard serve as the single source of truth—if a team member or advisor needs to know what happened in any given week, the answer is there. The tracker is now built out through Week 8 with detailed data for Weeks 1–7. On the compliance side, the three core researchers (Neelima, Kefei, John) have been consistently submitting, while Andrew and Aparna have remained absent throughout. My role has been effective at creating structure and visibility, but I recognize I have limited leverage over members who are disengaged from the project entirely.

## What challenges and successes have you observed in your group?

**Successes:** The research has progressed substantially. The team has moved from initial literature review and data exploration in Weeks 1–2 to producing real analytical results—Neelima's IUCN data cleanup (303 to 142 species) and overlap ratio metric, Kefei's merged trait datasets and species accumulation curve visualizations, and John's species-level detectability analysis showing precision of 0.852 vs. recall of 0.486. The transition to student-led meetings after Dr. McGuire went on sabbatical has worked well, and the team has maintained momentum without direct faculty oversight.

**Challenges:** The most persistent challenge is inconsistent participation. Andrew and Aparna have not submitted any weekly reports across all eight weeks tracked so far, and there has been no indication of when or whether they will re-engage. This creates a gap in the project's coverage and puts more load on the three active members. A secondary challenge is that report submissions occasionally come in late or in inconsistent formats, which adds friction to the integration process.

## How are you collaborating with stakeholders to ensure your role is supporting and motivating the group?

My primary stakeholder interactions are with Jacquie Carroll (computational advisor), who assigns weekly tasks, and the research team members themselves. I ensure the assigned tasks from Jacquie are clearly captured and distributed in each week's tracker sheet so the team has no ambiguity about expectations. For the researchers, the tracker serves as both an accountability mechanism and a record of their contributions—they can see their work documented and credited week over week. With Dr. McGuire on sabbatical and the team running student-led meetings, I've focused on making the operational side as low-friction as possible so the researchers can focus on the science. I also coordinate with Dr. Mussmann as needed for broader project guidance.

## What feedback have you received on your role, solicited or unsolicited?

Feedback has been mostly implicit rather than formal. The team has adopted the tracker as the standard workflow—researchers submit their reports knowing they'll be integrated, and Jacquie's task assignments flow through the same structure. The fact that the system is being used consistently by the active members is itself a form of positive feedback. I have not yet conducted a formal solicitation of feedback on my role, which is something I plan to do in the second half of the semester to identify any friction points I may not be seeing.

## What plans do you have for enhancing your role as the project manager with the remaining time in the semester?

For the remainder of the semester, I plan to: (1) formally solicit feedback from team members on what's working and what could be improved in the operations workflow; (2) address the Andrew/Aparna situation more directly, either by escalating to the advisor or documenting a decision to proceed without them; (3) continue building out weekly tracker sheets as reports come in, maintaining the Dashboard as the authoritative submission record; and (4) begin preparing a semester-end summary that captures the full arc of the project's progress and any lessons learned from the operations side.

## Please provide feedback on what support you may need in your role.

The main support I could use is clearer guidance on how to handle persistently absent team members. Andrew and Aparna have not engaged at all, and as a peer coordinator rather than a faculty supervisor, I have limited authority to compel participation. Guidance from the course or faculty advisors on whether to formally escalate, restructure the team's scope, or simply document the gap would help me make the right call. Beyond that, the operational tooling and workflow have been working well and I feel well-supported.
